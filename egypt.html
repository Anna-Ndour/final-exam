Ancient Egypt

The Egyptian Empire mainly refers to the New Kingdom period (c. 1570–1069 BCE), when Egypt reached its peak of power, wealth, and territorial expansion.

During this era, Egypt controlled lands from Nubia in the south to parts of the Levant in the north and became one of the most influential civilizations in the ancient world.

Geography

Ancient Egypt was located in northeastern Africa and was centered around the Nile River. The river provided fertile land, water, and transportation, making agriculture and trade possible.

Natural barriers such as deserts and seas helped protect Egypt from invasions and contributed to its long-term stability.

Key Periods of Egyptian Power

Old Kingdom (c. 2686–2181 BCE): Era of pyramid building and centralized rule.

Middle Kingdom (c. 2055–1650 BCE): Period of reunification and cultural flourishing.

New Kingdom (c. 1550–1070 BCE): The peak of Egyptian power, marked by territorial expansion and famous pharaohs.

Major Pharaohs

Hatshepsut

Hatshepsut was one of the most successful female pharaohs. She ruled peacefully and focused on trade, architecture, and economic prosperity.

Tutankhamun

Tutankhamon is famous for his richly decorated tomb. Although his reign was short, his discovery revealed much about ancient Egyptian culture.

Ramesses II

Ramesses II, also known as Ramesses the Great, was one of Egypt’s most powerful pharaohs, known for military campaigns and monumental architecture.

Decline and Foreign Rule

After the New Kingdom, Egypt faced internal instability and foreign invasions. It was ruled by Assyrians, Persians, Greeks, and Romans.

In 30 BCE, Egypt was conquered by Rome, marking the end of ancient Egyptian rule.
