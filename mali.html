Mali Empire

L'Empire du Mali (v. 1235 – v. 1670) était l'un des États les plus vastes et les plus riches de l'histoire de l'Afrique de l'Ouest, célèbre pour son contrôle du commerce de l'or et du sel.

Fondé par Soundiata Keïta après sa victoire à la bataille de Kirina en 1235, l'empire atteint son âge d'or sous Mansa Moussa (v. 1312–1337), souvent considéré comme l'homme le plus riche de l'histoire.

Tombouctou et Djenné devinrent des centres mondiaux de commerce, de culture islamique et d'éducation, notamment grâce à l'Université de Sankoré.

Geography

The Mali Empire was located in West Africa and controlled important trade routes. Its territory included rivers, savannas, and desert areas, allowing strong economic exchanges between North and Sub-Saharan Africa.

Kings and Leaders

Soundiata Keita

Founder of the Mali Empire, Soundiata Keita unified the Mandinka people after his victory at the Battle of Kirina in 1235. He established strong political foundations through the Charter of Manden, one of the oldest constitutions in the world.

Mansa Musa

Mansa Musa ruled the Mali Empire during its golden age. He is famous for his immense wealth, his pilgrimage to Mecca, and for promoting education, trade, and Islamic culture. Under his reign, cities like Timbuktu became major centers of learning.

Soumaoro Kanté

Soumaoro Kanté was the king of the Sosso Kingdom and the main rival of Soundiata Keita. He was defeated at the Battle of Kirina in 1235, an event that led to the foundation of the Mali Empire. He is often remembered as a powerful and feared warrior king.

Decline

The empire began to decline in the 15th century due to internal conflicts and the rise of the Songhai Empire, before fragmenting in the 17th century.
